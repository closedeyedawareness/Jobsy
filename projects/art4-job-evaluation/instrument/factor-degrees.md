# Factor degree levels — DRAFT for review

**Status: draft, 2026-07-21.** Degree definitions only — deliberately NO weighting yet
(see README, "Design order"). Written from scratch against Art. 4's four factors; not
derived from any protected instrument.

Each factor is rated on 6 degrees. Six because our grade ladder spans 14 grades across
very different work (intern → C-suite): fewer degrees can't separate the middle, more
invites false precision. Open question for review: whether Working Conditions needs all
6 or collapses to 4 in practice for office-heavy populations.

---

## Factor 1 — Skills (kennis en vaardigheden)

What the job requires the holder to know and be able to do — not what the current holder
happens to know. Evidence base: `role_skill_map` required levels, required_skills,
description.

| Degree | Definition |
|---|---|
| 1 | Task competence learnable on the job in weeks; no prior domain knowledge assumed |
| 2 | Working command of one domain's routine methods; typically MBO-level or equivalent experience |
| 3 | Full professional command of one domain incl. non-routine cases; HBO/WO entry or equivalent |
| 4 | Deep specialisation in one domain OR working command of several adjacent domains; keeps methods current independently |
| 5 | Recognised expert; the org's reference point in the domain; develops methods others follow |
| 6 | Shapes the discipline itself across the organisation (or beyond); rare, strategic expertise |

## Factor 2 — Effort (inspanning)

The intensity and character of the mental, emotional and (where applicable) physical
demand — sustained concentration, problem-solving under uncertainty, emotional load from
the work itself. **Deliberately includes emotional load as first-class effort** — its
historic omission is one of the documented gender-bias failure modes in job evaluation.

| Degree | Definition |
|---|---|
| 1 | Routine attention; interruptions cost little; low emotional demand |
| 2 | Sustained attention in stretches; occasional deadline pressure |
| 3 | Regular deep concentration OR regular emotionally demanding interactions (conflict, distress, high-stakes clients) |
| 4 | Frequent complex problem-solving under time pressure OR sustained emotional load as a core feature of the work |
| 5 | Continuous high cognitive load with material consequence of lapses; or intense emotional demand with little recovery time |
| 6 | Extreme, sustained demand few can carry long-term; the org structures rotation/support around it |

## Factor 3 — Responsibility (verantwoordelijkheid)

Scope of decisions owned, resources controlled, consequence of error, accountability for
people. Evidence base: `management_level`, `key_responsibilities`, JobGrade
authority text.

| Degree | Definition |
|---|---|
| 1 | Own task quality; errors caught quickly nearby; no budget, no reports |
| 2 | Own workstream; errors surface downstream at real but recoverable cost; may coordinate others informally |
| 3 | Owns a process or project end-to-end; measurable budget/asset responsibility; may lead 1–5 people |
| 4 | Owns a domain/department outcome; sets priorities within mandate; leads a team or carries equivalent expert accountability |
| 5 | Owns a function; decisions move organisation-level results; accountable for multiple teams or org-critical assets |
| 6 | Enterprise accountability; decisions bind the organisation externally |

**Note:** "or carries equivalent expert accountability" in degree 4 is deliberate — 
responsibility must not be readable only through headcount managed, which structurally
undervalues senior expert (often mixed-gender) tracks against managerial (historically
male) tracks.

## Factor 4 — Working conditions (arbeidsomstandigheden)

The circumstances the work imposes: physical environment, hazard, irregularity,
travel, on-call. Rated on the JOB's normal conditions, not incidents.

| Degree | Definition |
|---|---|
| 1 | Controlled indoor environment, regular hours, no meaningful hazard |
| 2 | Mostly controlled; occasional travel, occasional evening/weekend peaks |
| 3 | Regular travel OR structurally irregular hours OR regular exposure to demanding physical/sensory circumstances |
| 4 | Frequent site/field work with real physical demand or hazard controls; or structural on-call |
| 5 | Predominantly demanding/hazard-managed environments; irregularity shapes private life |
| 6 | Sustained hazardous or extreme conditions as the core context of the job |

## Review questions (for the weighting session)

1. Does 6 degrees hold for Working Conditions given an office-heavy population, or
   does sparse use of degrees 4–6 argue for a 4-degree scale there?
2. Effort degree 3 bundles cognitive and emotional demand with "OR" — should they be
   split into separately-rated sub-dimensions to keep emotional load from being absorbed?
3. Skills degree definitions lean on Dutch qualification anchors (MBO/HBO/WO) — keep
   (recognisable to NL clients) or abstract (portable internationally)?
